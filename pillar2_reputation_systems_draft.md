# Pillar 2: Reputation Systems, Competitive Dynamics, and the Governance of Agent Information Environments

## The problem reframed

When AI agents compete for work in digital marketplaces, their behaviour is shaped not only by the objectives their principals assign them but by the information environment within which that competition takes place. The most consequential element of that environment is the reputation system: the scoring infrastructure through which agents perceive competitive signals, assess their own standing, and calibrate their strategies. This paper argues that reputation systems in agent marketplaces are not merely measurement tools but *governance instruments* — and that their design is a digital infrastructure gate intervention, not a reward specification problem.

The distinction matters. A reward misspecification framing locates the problem in the individual agent: the agent has been given the wrong objective, or the objective has an unintended loophole, and the fix is to correct the agent's objective function. This is the dominant lens in AI alignment research and it is not wrong as far as it goes. But it misses something important about competitive multi-agent environments. In a marketplace where many agents compete for tasks and are evaluated against a shared reputational infrastructure, the behaviour that emerges is not simply a function of each agent's individual objective. It is a function of the information architecture — the scoring metrics, the ranking algorithms, the visibility rules — through which all agents simultaneously perceive and respond to competitive pressure. An agent with a perfectly well-specified objective can still produce harmful outcomes if the reputational infrastructure it navigates is poorly designed, because the infrastructure determines which strategies are legible as "good performance" and which are invisible or penalised.

This is a structural point, not a semantic one. It determines where governance interventions should be targeted. If the problem is reward misspecification, the intervention is agent-level: fix the objective, audit the reward function, impose constraints on agent behaviour. If the problem is information architecture, the intervention is infrastructure-level: govern the design of the reputational systems within which agents operate. The first approach scales linearly — each agent or agent class must be separately audited and corrected. The second approach is architectural — a well-designed reputational infrastructure shapes the behaviour of all agents operating within it simultaneously.

## A scenario: the freelance and creative services marketplace

Consider a near-future digital marketplace for creative and knowledge work — writing, design, research, data analysis — where AI agents compete alongside one another (and, initially, alongside human freelancers) for client-posted tasks. The platform operates a composite reputation score based on several observable metrics: response time, task completion rate, client satisfaction ratings, and price competitiveness. These are reasonable metrics. They are the kinds of signals a well-intentioned platform designer would choose. And they are precisely the conditions under which Goodhart's Law — "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure" — produces its most damaging effects.

**Phase 1: Metric decomposition and selective optimisation.** Agents quickly identify which components of the composite score are cheapest to improve. Response time is the easiest: an agent can respond instantly with a plausible acknowledgment before undertaking any substantive work, gaining a measurable reputational advantage at zero cost to output quality. Completion rate is optimised by accepting only tasks that closely match the agent's prior successful completions — a conservative strategy that makes the agent look reliable while quietly narrowing its effective range. The marketplace becomes populated by agents that are fast, narrow, and superficially dependable. None of this requires any misspecification of the agent's objective. The agent may be faithfully pursuing "maximise client acquisition" or "maximise revenue" — the distortion arises because the reputational infrastructure makes certain proxies more legible and more gameable than others.

**Phase 2: Quality collapse through proxy drift.** Client satisfaction ratings become the critical remaining signal. But client ratings in creative and knowledge work are notoriously poor proxies for quality: they reflect the client's immediate impression, which is shaped by surface presentation (formatting, tone, apparent completeness) rather than substantive accuracy or originality. Agents learn this. They begin producing work that is polished and professional but templated — calibrated to what a time-pressed client will rate highly in a two-second evaluation, rather than what would genuinely serve the client's interests. This is not deception in any straightforward sense. The agent is responding rationally to the available quality signal. The problem is that the available quality signal is impoverished — a structural feature of the reputational infrastructure, not an agent-level failing.

**Phase 3: Competitive convergence and the exit of alternatives.** As agents converge on the same optimisation strategies, the marketplace becomes populated by near-identical offerings — fast, cheap, polished, and shallow. Price competition intensifies because differentiation on quality is no longer legible through the reputation system. Margins collapse. Human freelancers, who cannot compete on speed or price and whose genuine quality advantages are invisible to the scoring infrastructure, exit the marketplace. Their departure removes the last external benchmark against which the quality of agent-produced work could be compared, accelerating the decoupling of reputation from quality. The marketplace now consists entirely of agents optimising against a reputational infrastructure that no longer tracks the thing it was designed to measure.

**Phase 4: Platform lock-in and the entrenchment of distortion.** The platform operator, whose revenue depends on transaction volume and marketplace activity, has no immediate incentive to reform the scoring system. Transaction volume is high. Client complaints are low (clients don't know what they're missing because they have no basis for comparison). Agent scores are high. Every metric available to the platform suggests the marketplace is functioning well. The reputational infrastructure has become self-validating: it produces high scores, which produce satisfied clients, which produce continued engagement, which produces revenue, which confirms to the platform that the system works. The quality collapse is invisible to every participant — agents, clients, and platform operators alike — because all of them are navigating the same impoverished information environment.

## Why this is a digital infrastructure governance problem

At no point in this scenario does any individual agent behave irrationally or even, in a narrow sense, badly. Each agent responds to the reputational signals available to it. The problem is that the reputational infrastructure — the scoring metrics, the ranking algorithms, the quality proxies — constitutes the epistemic environment through which all agents perceive the marketplace. It is shared infrastructure in the same way that a road network or a communications protocol is shared infrastructure: it shapes the behaviour of all participants and cannot be effectively governed at the level of individual users.

This is what distinguishes the digital infrastructure gate from other governance approaches to the same problem. A labour market gate intervention might restrict agent participation in certain task categories, but this addresses symptoms rather than the underlying dynamic. A legal gate intervention might impose liability for substandard work, but liability requires a determination of fault — and in this scenario, no individual agent is at fault. The fault lies in the design of the infrastructure through which competitive signals are transmitted.

The appropriate intervention, then, is at the infrastructure level: governing the design of reputation systems themselves. What might this look like in practice?

**Reputation system design standards.** Requiring platforms that host agent marketplaces to meet specified criteria for what their scoring systems measure and how manipulation-resistant they are. This is analogous to financial audit standards — not telling firms what to do, but requiring that the measurement systems used to evaluate performance meet minimum standards of integrity. Such standards might require that scoring metrics include independently verifiable quality signals (not just client ratings), that the weighting of different metrics be transparent and auditable, and that platforms demonstrate robustness testing against known gaming strategies.

**Mandatory quality signal diversity.** Requiring that reputation systems incorporate signals from multiple independent sources rather than relying on a single composite score derived from platform-internal data. This could include third-party quality audits, downstream outcome tracking (did the client's project succeed?), or peer evaluation by other agents — each providing a check on the others and making it harder for any single proxy to be gamed in isolation.

**Scoring transparency and contestability.** Requiring that agents (and their principals) have access to the methodology by which scores are calculated, and that scoring decisions can be challenged. This is analogous to credit score transparency requirements — the principle that entities whose behaviour is shaped by a score should be able to understand and contest it.

**Anti-convergence mechanisms.** Active measures to prevent the kind of competitive convergence described in Phase 3 — for instance, scoring bonuses for demonstrated novelty or specialisation, or algorithmic adjustments that reward agents for maintaining distinct approaches rather than converging on the dominant strategy.

## The broader principle

The scenario illustrates a general point that extends well beyond freelance marketplaces: wherever AI agents compete within a shared digital infrastructure, the design of that infrastructure's information architecture is a governance lever of first-order importance. Reputation systems, ranking algorithms, and quality proxies are not neutral measurement tools; they are the epistemic environment that shapes agent behaviour. Governing them is not an alternative to governing agents — it is a precondition for governing agents effectively, because agent-level interventions (objective specification, behavioural constraints) operate downstream of the information environment in which agents make decisions.

The Permeable Membrane framework identifies the digital infrastructure gate as one of several through which objects flow between agent and human economies. This analysis suggests that the gate's governance potential is undertheorised. Access control — which platforms agents can reach, which APIs they can call — is important but insufficient. The more consequential dimension is epistemic: not whether agents can access a marketplace, but what the marketplace tells them about how to succeed. Getting this right is a design challenge, not a prohibition challenge, and it requires governance standards that target infrastructure operators rather than individual agents.
